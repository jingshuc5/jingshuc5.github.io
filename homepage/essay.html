Invasive Color

My neighborhood is changing.

It’s also judgmental.

How can anyone even stand this arrogance?

In a small corner of the Lower East Side, at the foot of the Manhattan Bridge, lies a neighborhood where you can find the best dumplings, lo mein, and experience Asian culture. That’s what we have always been known for. It’s either that or we’re known for our black hair and almond-shaped eyes. We are also known for our stinky attitude and arrogance in thinking we hold more knowledge than the average American-born Chinese.

It’s crazy that there haven’t been any quarrels over this arrogance. This attitude is like a social norm that you know but will never say out loud, like seeing an old Asian grandma taking up so much space in the subway with her five big red plastic bags or a middle-aged man who’s mumbling to himself. At the same time, he tries to figure out how to use a flip phone to dial his son on the bus, or Asian shops that judge every customer who walks through their door. Many just give them an annoyed look, but we never say anything about it.

This arrogance also shows itself in rude Asian ladies who run a restaurant, yelling orders across the room. Or the nasty look they give to the various news on the television and modern teenagers who think they know it all. Or in the way that they don’t know how to take their days off, since they always want to be working. Or in the way my mother would never want to take a day off. Or in the way that they will never listen to anyone and don’t care about what people say about them. Or in the way they’re so stubborn.

This is only one part of the people in Chinatown. The other group of people is the nice ones: the ones who offer fruits and drinks when you visit their home, give you a bright smile when you hold the door for them, and tap their kid who can speak English to express their gratitude to you. Most often, the previous group of people were once the nice ones until they were sick of the nasty attitude that they got from the people who are not Chinese. They are perceived as stupid when they’re being nice and dumb when they don’t understand the social trends and norms in the area. When my grandmother and mom gave kindness to others, they only received a response of “Haha! She doesn’t get it.”

These are my people. This is the place where I belong. My life revolves around this area and has revolved around it even when my family and I lived in Brooklyn. Maybe it’s because I’m Chinese, and that creates a sense of familiarity and belonging. This neighborhood has given me a backbone of attitude I didn’t know I had. The neighborhood’s colorful attitude and resilience had bled onto my naive plain shirt that I put on when I stepped into the so-called land of dreams and freedom: America.

Will the neighborhood’s attitude ever disappear in the future? I don’t know. If I leave this place in two years and come back after another four years, will I still call this place my home? Or would it be unrecognizable? If this question were asked to me a couple of years ago, I would’ve said that this resilience will always be here, no matter what. Now this community has changed. There is now a new group of people with new colors of attitudes. The colors of Western tourism and the colors of American-born Chinese. Will the invasive colors of Western tourism and American-born Chinese take up territory in this small, crowded space? Will they cover up and swallow my home? I don’t know.

I guess there’s something attractive about this space, and that’s why the invasive colors keep coming back. Maybe the food is so good to the point where they keep coming back, the cute trinkets of makeup and keychains that they see as “Asian aesthetics,” or they want to experience Asian culture once in a while. I guess that’s also a bit extreme. I mean, the food is good. I love the food that this neighborhood serves. Beyond the dumplings, sesame chicken, dim sum, and lo mein, there is phở, fried squid, luo si fen (stinky and spicy noodles), stinky tofu that you find in street carts, white fluffy cake with sesame seeds that live in my childhood, pig blood, and sweetened milk that my mom would always buy for me when I get a good test score. Maybe it’s the cute souvenirs that many Asian communities sell that they always come back for, such as Legos, Asian snacks, cute stationery, and detailed jewelry.

However, the average tourist to this neighborhood just stops at the surface. They never go any deeper than that. They will never try my childhood foods and toys because they mentally label them as weird. These people include those like a group of American white families that I can always find in Asian dim sum restaurants ordering dumplings and lo mein, teenagers who believe in getting boba and cute trending desserts will make them more related to the culture, groups of American friends that I see now and then getting told that this restaurant does not serve dumpling and dim sum and that they only serve noodles and BBQ. As the famous saying goes: They are barely scratching the surface.

The invasive colors of this neighborhood might change this place and I’ll stop recognizing the place where I get my favorite snack after school, the aunties who greet me when I get a new bag of frozen meat buns, or the eye roll that I see from grandmas and grandpas observing the changing world around them.

My neighborhood is changing. For better or worse.
